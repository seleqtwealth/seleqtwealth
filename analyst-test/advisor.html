Welcome. This is a practical assessment, not a textbook or CFP-style exam. We are not testing recall of rules or formulas. We want to see how you think as a planner and adviser, about clients, suitability, and the trade-offs in real situations.

The last section, On the Job, puts you in real advisory situations and asks for your judgment: identify the issue and the right course of action for the client. There are no trick questions and no negative marking, and no calculations need a calculator. If you are unsure, give your best practical reasoning rather than leaving it blank.

Take your time. Aim for roughly 30 minutes. When you are done, press Finish & Lock at the bottom.
Section A

Planning Fundamentals & Goals

4 questions
A1SuitabilityTrue / False · why

If a client walks in already certain which fund they want, it is fine to simply act on that instruction without first assessing their goals and risk profile.

Answer
False. A planner has a duty of suitability, you still have to understand the client's goals, horizon and risk and check the product actually fits, even on a direct instruction (and document it). Acting blind on instruction is how mis-buying happens. Look for: picks False and names suitability / duty-of-care before acting.
A2Risk profiling · adviseWritten

A client insists he is an aggressive investor and wants to go fully into equity, but the money in question is for buying a home in about 2 years. What should drive your recommendation, and why?

What to look for
Risk capacity (set by the short, fixed 2-year horizon) overrides his stated tolerance, equity simply can't be relied on over 2 years, so recommend capital preservation (liquid / short-duration debt / FD). Reward naming the capacity-vs-tolerance distinction and prioritising the goal's horizon over the client's self-label.
A3Goal-basedMultiple choice

A client has three goals: a 6-month emergency buffer, a car in 3 years, and retirement in 25 years. Which approach fits best?

Correct answer
B. Each goal's time horizon should drive its own asset mix (bucketing). A single blanket portfolio (A) either over-risks the near-term money or under-grows the long-term money; C over-risks the buffer and car; D starves the retirement goal of growth.
A4Real valueMultiple choice

You're sizing the corpus a client needs for a child's college in 15 years. Which is the soundest basis?

Correct answer
B. Education inflation is high, so you must grow today's fee to its future value. A and C badly understate the goal; D confuses how much they can invest with how much the goal will cost.
Section B

Retirement, Insurance & Risk

4 questions
B1ProtectionMultiple choice

A 32-year-old sole earner with two dependents wants the largest income protection his budget allows. Which gives the most cover for the lowest premium?

Correct answer
C. Pure protection is cheapest through term; the others (A, B, D) all bundle an investment into the premium, which buys far less cover per rupee. Separate protection from investing.
B2AdequacyMultiple choice

How would you arrive at how much life cover a working client needs?

Correct answer
B. Cover should replace lost income and clear liabilities (an income-replacement / needs basis, ~10–15× income, plus loans, minus existing assets). A, C and D are convenient shortcuts that routinely leave families under-covered.
B3Health riskTrue / False · why

A client's employer-provided group health cover typically ends when they leave or change that job.

Answer
True. Group cover is tied to the employer and lapses when the job does, and it's often inadequate. That is exactly why a client should also hold a personal health policy, for continuity and to lock in insurability before age or illness make cover costly. Look for: True + recognising the job-tied risk and why a personal policy matters.
B4Retirement · adviseWritten

A client has just retired and will draw a monthly income from their investment corpus. Of everything that could go wrong, which single risk would worry you most in the first few years, why, and how would you guard against it?

What to look for
Sequence-of-returns risk, a large fall early in retirement, while money is being withdrawn, can permanently impair the corpus. Guard against it with a cash/debt buffer of a few years' withdrawals (a bucket strategy) so equity isn't sold in a downturn, plus a conservative early glide path. Reward naming sequence risk and a concrete buffer/bucket mitigation.
Section C

Tax, Estate & Succession

4 questions
C1Tax efficiencyTrue / False · why

As long as a client stays within equity funds, frequently switching between them to chase the best performer carries no real tax cost.

Answer
False. Each switch is a redemption, it can realise taxable capital gains and trigger exit loads and costs. Staying invested defers tax and lets it compound. Look for: False + realised-gains / tax-drag reasoning.
C2Tax-savingMultiple choice

A 30-year-old in the 30% tax bracket wants an 80C investment that also builds long-term wealth. Which fits best?

Correct answer
B. For tax saving and growth with the shortest lock-in, ELSS fits. PPF is safe but long and debt-like; the FD's returns are modest and taxed; the endowment bundles insurance and savings poorly. The candidate has to weigh growth vs lock-in vs suitability, not spot a joke.
C3SuccessionTrue / False · why

The nominee on a client's mutual fund or bank account automatically becomes the legal owner of those assets when the client dies.

Answer
False. A nominee is a trustee/custodian who receives the assets to pass on to the legal heirs; who actually inherits is decided by a will or succession law. A common and important client misconception. Look for: False + the nominee-vs-heir distinction.
C4Estate planningMultiple choice

An HNI family wants their wealth to pass to the next generation smoothly, privately and with minimal disputes. Beyond holding assets jointly, the most robust step is:

Correct answer
B. A valid will is the base; for a complex/large estate a family trust adds control, privacy and continuity and pre-empts disputes. A, C and D are partial measures with real gaps (nominations don't decide ownership; gifting loses control and has tax angles; a letter of wishes isn't legally binding).
Section D

Products, Suitability & Regulation

3 questions
D1MFD vs RIAWritten

In your own words, what is the difference between a mutual-fund distributor (MFD) and a SEBI-registered investment adviser (RIA), and why does that distinction matter to a client?

What to look for
An MFD earns commission from the product makers for distribution; an RIA charges the client a fee and advises in a fiduciary capacity, with advice and distribution kept separate. Why it matters: how the adviser is paid shapes potential conflicts of interest, the client should know whose interest the advice serves. Reward grasping commission-vs-fee, the fiduciary duty, and the conflict angle (especially for a firm that is both).
D2CostsMultiple choice

A client asks whether to hold the Direct or Regular plan of the same mutual fund. Which is accurate?

Correct answer
B. Same portfolio, but Direct carries no trail commission, so a lower expense ratio and a slightly higher net return. A, C and D are common misconceptions.
D3PortfolioTrue / False · why

Rebalancing a portfolio is mainly about keeping its risk aligned with the plan, rather than about maximising returns.

Answer
True. Rebalancing controls risk drift, keeping the mix aligned with the plan, and instils a sell-high / buy-low discipline. It is not a return-maximiser, and rebalancing too often just adds cost and tax. Look for: True + risk-control reasoning (ideally noting that more-often isn't better).
Section E

On the Job

5 advisory situations
E1Suitability · adviseWritten

A client with a balanced, goal-aligned portfolio wants to move 60% of it into one red-hot sectoral fund that's up sharply this year. As their adviser, how do you respond, and what is the right course of action?

What to look for
Acknowledge the appeal, then the problems: heavy concentration in one sector, performance-chasing / recency bias, and it breaks the goal-aligned plan and pushes risk past the client's profile. Right course: revisit goals and risk, stay diversified, at most a small satellite (say 5–10%) if it genuinely fits, then rebalance. Reward suitability-first reasoning and a concrete moderate alternative, not a flat "no".
E2Retirement · adviseWritten

A 57-year-old retiring in 3 years has their entire savings in fixed deposits because "FDs are safe." What is the planning problem, and what would you recommend?

What to look for
Problem: "safe" only in nominal terms, over a 25–30 year retirement, inflation plus tax leaves a near-zero or negative real return (longevity/inflation risk). Recommend: a glide-path / bucket approach, keep near-term needs safe but add a growth sleeve for the long tail, diversify, plan withdrawals. Reward naming inflation/longevity risk and a balanced, staged mix rather than 100% FD or 100% equity.
E3Protection · diagnoseWritten

A 35-year-old sole earner has a ₹50 lakh home loan, two young children, no term life cover, and is putting all surplus into equity SIPs. What is the most important gap, and what do you do first?

What to look for
The glaring gap is protection: if the earner dies, the family is left with the loan and no income, and the SIPs won't have grown enough to cover it. Protection comes before wealth-building. First: adequate term life cover (loan + income replacement + goals), plus health cover and an emergency fund, then continue the SIPs. Reward putting risk management ahead of returns.
E4Ethics · adviseWritten

Your firm earns a much higher commission on Product X, but Product Y is cheaper and a better fit for this client's goal. The client trusts your recommendation completely. What do you do, and why?

What to look for · character check
Recommend the suitable option (Y), the client's interest and trust come first, and be transparent about how you're paid. Never let commission drive advice, that's mis-selling. Reward clear fiduciary/ethical reasoning, awareness of the MFD vs RIA conflict, and recognition that the client's trust is the firm's real long-term asset. A weak or self-serving answer here is a serious red flag.
E5Build a plan · capstoneWritten

A new client, 40, married with two young children, a home loan, a handful of mutual funds bought ad hoc, and one insurance policy from years ago, says: "I have no idea if I'm on track. Sort me out." How would you approach building their financial plan, and in what order?

What to look for · planner's mindset
A logical sequence, not a jump to product picks. First understand them (goals, cash flow, net worth, risk profile). Then the foundation, emergency fund and protection (adequate term + health) before chasing growth. Review the old policy for suitability (replace if it's a poor product), and consolidate the ad-hoc funds into a goal-based, diversified allocation. Then document the plan and set a review cadence. Reward the assess → protect → build order and breadth.
