Welcome. This is a practical assessment, not a textbook or CFP-style exam. We are not testing recall of rules or formulas. We want to see how you think as a planner and adviser, about clients, suitability, and the trade-offs in real situations.

The last section, On the Job, puts you in real advisory situations and asks for your judgment: identify the issue and the right course of action for the client. There are no trick questions and no negative marking, and no calculations need a calculator. If you are unsure, give your best practical reasoning rather than leaving it blank.

Take your time. Aim for roughly 30 minutes. When you are done, press Finish & Lock at the bottom.
Section A

Planning Fundamentals & Goals

4 questions
A1Planning processMultiple choice

A new client opens with "just tell me which funds to buy." Before recommending any product, the first thing a planner should establish is:

Correct answer
B. Products come last. Planning starts with understanding the client, goals, horizon, capacity and cash flow, then suitability, then product. Picking a product first is the classic mistake.
A2Risk profilingMultiple choice

A client insists he is "aggressive" and wants full equity, but he needs this specific money in 2 years for a home purchase. What should govern the recommendation?

Correct answer
B. Distinguish risk capacity (ability to take risk, set by horizon and finances) from tolerance (willingness). A short, fixed horizon means low capacity, which must win. Strong sign if the candidate names that distinction.
A3Goal-basedMultiple choice

A client has three goals: an emergency buffer, a car in 3 years, and retirement in 25 years. The soundest approach is:

Correct answer
B. Goal bucketing: each goal's time horizon drives its own asset mix. One blanket portfolio either takes too much risk with near-term money or too little with long-term money.
A4Real valueMultiple choice

When estimating the corpus needed for a child's education 15 years away, ignoring inflation would:

Correct answer
B. Education and medical inflation are high in India; you must grow today's cost to the future value. Planning on today's fees badly under-funds the goal. Tests the real-vs-nominal instinct.
Section B

Retirement, Insurance & Risk

4 questions
B1ProtectionMultiple choice

For pure income protection of a 32-year-old sole earner with two dependents, the most cost-effective cover is:

Correct answer
C. Keep protection and investment separate, term gives the most cover per rupee. Bundled products (endowment/ULIP/money-back) dilute both the cover and the returns.
B2AdequacyMultiple choice

A common starting rule of thumb for how much life cover a working person needs is roughly:

Correct answer
B. Roughly 10–15× income as a start (or a proper income-replacement / human-life-value / needs basis). Bonus if they add outstanding liabilities and future goals, and net off existing assets.
B3Health riskMultiple choice

Why advise a client to hold a personal health policy even though their employer already provides group cover?

Correct answer
A. Group cover is tied to the job, can be withdrawn, and is often too small. A personal policy ensures continuity and locks in insurability/renewal history early.
B4Drawdown riskMultiple choice

For a client who has just retired and is drawing an income from their corpus, the most damaging event is:

Correct answer
B. Sequence-of-returns risk: a big early drawdown combined with withdrawals is very hard to recover from. Mitigated by a cash/debt buffer (bucket strategy) and a conservative early-retirement glide path.
Section C

Tax, Estate & Succession

4 questions
C1Tax efficiencyMultiple choice

Frequently switching equity funds to chase performance is usually tax-inefficient because:

Correct answer
B. Churning realises gains (tax now) and adds costs; staying invested defers tax and lets it compound. No exact rates needed, the principle is the point.
C2Tax-savingMultiple choice

A young client in a high tax bracket wants to save tax under 80C and build long-term wealth. Among common 80C options, the best fit is usually:

Correct answer
B. Match the tool to goal and horizon: ELSS gives growth potential with the shortest 80C lock-in. Buying insurance purely for a tax deduction (D) is a classic mis-step.
C3SuccessionMultiple choice

A client believes the nominee on their mutual funds and bank accounts automatically becomes the owner of those assets on death. In fact:

Correct answer
B. A nominee is a custodian/trustee, not the owner; a valid will (or succession law) decides who inherits. A frequent and important client misconception, good for an adviser to catch.
C4Estate planningMultiple choice

For an HNI family wanting a smooth, private, dispute-resistant transfer of assets across generations, a useful structure beyond a basic will is:

Correct answer
B. A will is the base; for larger/complex estates a family trust adds control, privacy, continuity and helps avoid probate disputes. Directly relevant to SELEQT's HNI clientele.
Section D

Products, Suitability & Regulation

4 questions
D1MFD vs RIAMultiple choice

The core difference between a mutual-fund distributor (MFD) and a SEBI-registered investment adviser (RIA) is:

Correct answer
B. Commission vs fee, and the RIA's fiduciary duty with advice/distribution segregated. Central to a firm that is both MFD and RIA. Strong sign if the candidate grasps the conflict-of-interest angle.
D2CostsMultiple choice

Compared with a Regular plan, a Direct plan of the same mutual fund:

Correct answer
B. Direct = no trail commission → lower expense ratio → higher net return, same underlying portfolio. (Worth knowing where it fits given the MFD/RIA models.)
D3EthicsMultiple choice

A product that pays you a high commission doesn't fit the client's goal, but a lower-paying option does. The right action is:

Correct answer
B. Suitability and the client's interest come first; disclose conflicts. A direct integrity check, central to the role and the firm's reputation.
D4PortfolioMultiple choice

Periodically rebalancing a portfolio back to its target asset mix:

Correct answer
B. Rebalancing controls risk drift and instils contrarian discipline. It is not a return guarantee (C is the trap).
Section E

On the Job

6 advisory situations
E1Suitability · adviseWritten

A client with a balanced, goal-aligned portfolio wants to move 60% of it into one red-hot sectoral fund that's up sharply this year. As their adviser, how do you respond, and what is the right course of action?

What to look for
Acknowledge the appeal, then the problems: heavy concentration in one sector, performance-chasing / recency bias, and it breaks the goal-aligned plan and pushes risk past the client's profile. Right course: revisit goals and risk, stay diversified, and at most a small satellite (say 5–10%) if it genuinely fits, then rebalance. Reward suitability-first reasoning and a concrete moderate alternative, not just a flat "no".
E2Retirement · adviseWritten

A 57-year-old retiring in 3 years has their entire savings in fixed deposits because "FDs are safe." What is the planning problem, and what would you recommend?

What to look for
Problem: "safe" only in nominal terms, over a 25–30 year retirement, inflation plus tax leaves a near-zero or negative real return (longevity/inflation risk). Recommend: a glide-path / bucket approach, keep near-term needs safe but add a growth sleeve (equity-tilted) for the long tail, diversify, and plan withdrawals. Reward naming inflation/longevity risk and proposing a balanced, staged mix rather than 100% FD or 100% equity.
E3Protection · diagnoseWritten

A 35-year-old sole earner has a ₹50 lakh home loan, two young children, no term life cover, and is putting all surplus into equity SIPs. What is the most important gap, and what do you do first?

What to look for
The glaring gap is protection: if the earner dies, the family is left with the loan and no income, and the SIPs won't have grown enough to cover it. Protection comes before wealth-building. First: adequate term life cover (loan + income replacement + goals), plus health cover and an emergency fund, then continue the SIPs. Reward putting risk management ahead of returns.
E4Judgment · adviseWritten

A client realises a high-cost endowment/ULIP they were sold years ago is underperforming and wants to surrender it immediately. How should you analyse this before advising?

What to look for
Not a reflex "surrender" or "keep". Analyse: surrender value vs paid-up vs continuing; charges are largely sunk; years left to maturity; whether making it paid-up beats surrendering; the protection it provides (replace with term first if it was their only cover); and tax on surrender. Often the answer is replace with term + invest the difference, but only after checking the numbers and the protection gap. Reward a structured, numbers-and-protection-aware approach over a knee-jerk one.
E5Ethics · adviseWritten

Your firm earns a much higher commission on Product X, but Product Y is cheaper and a better fit for this client's goal. The client trusts your recommendation completely. What do you do, and why?

What to look for · character check
Recommend the suitable option (Y), the client's interest and trust come first, and be transparent about how you're paid. Never let commission drive advice, that's mis-selling. Reward clear fiduciary/ethical reasoning, awareness of the MFD vs RIA conflict, and recognition that the client's trust is the firm's real long-term asset. A weak or self-serving answer here is a serious red flag.
E6Succession · adviseWritten

An HNI client with assets spread across mutual funds, property and business interests, and a blended family, wants to ensure a smooth, fair transfer to the next generation. What should their plan include?

What to look for
A clear, valid will; nominations aligned with the will (not contradicting it); for the complexity, a family trust for control, privacy and continuity; liquidity planning for taxes/expenses on transfer; and clarity to pre-empt disputes (especially with a blended family), reviewed periodically. Reward a structured estate-planning answer touching will + nomination alignment + trust + liquidity, suited to an HNI context.
