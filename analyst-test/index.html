Welcome. This is a practical assessment, not a textbook exam. We are not looking for formulas or jargon. We want to understand how you think about money, markets, and clients in real situations.

The last section, On the Job, gives you real situations and asks for your analysis: identify the underlying issue and the right course of action, not how you would word it to a client. There are no trick questions and no negative marking. If you are unsure, give your best practical reasoning rather than leaving it blank.

Take your time. Aim for roughly 30 minutes. When you are done, press Finish & Lock at the bottom.
Section A

Markets & Instruments

5 questions
A1SuitabilityMultiple choice

A client has ₹10 lakh that she will need in about 8 months for a house down-payment. Where is the most suitable place to park this money?

Correct answer
B. The money is needed very soon, so capital safety and liquidity matter more than growth. Equity (A, C) can fall in the short term; ELSS (D) is locked for 3 years. Good sign if she reasons from the short time horizon.
A2Risk & returnMultiple choice

Over the long term, which of these typically carries the highest risk and the highest potential return?

Correct answer
C. Equity has the highest long-term risk and return. Understanding the risk–return ladder is core.
A3AppliedMultiple choice

A client has a monthly SIP running in an equity fund. The market then falls sharply for three months. Other things being equal, what is actually happening to her SIP during that fall?

Correct answer
B. A falling market means the same rupee buys more units, which lowers the average cost (rupee-cost averaging). Pausing in a downturn defeats the purpose. Strong sign if she'd reassure the client and keep the SIP going rather than stop it.
A4Bonds & ratesMultiple choice

When interest rates in the economy rise, the price of existing bonds (and the NAV of a debt fund holding them) generally:

Correct answer
B. Bond prices and interest rates move inversely, which is why even "safe" debt funds can dip when rates rise. One of the tougher ones; getting it right is a strong signal, getting it wrong is not a red flag for a non-finance background.
A5Fund selectionMultiple choice

A client wants to invest in a particular mutual fund only because it was the single best performer last year. What is the most sensible thing to tell her?

Correct answer
B. Chasing last year's winner is a classic mistake; past performance is not a guarantee. B is the balanced view (returns matter, but in the context of consistency, risk and goals). C and D are over-corrections.
Section B

Macro & Economy

4 questions
B1InstitutionsMultiple choice

Which institution sets the benchmark interest rate (the repo rate) in India?

Correct answer
B. The RBI is the central bank and sets the repo rate. (SEBI is the markets regulator — useful that she knows the difference.)
B2Cause & effectMultiple choice

If the RBI raises the repo rate, what is the most likely practical effect for clients?

Correct answer
A. Higher repo rate → costlier borrowing (loans, EMIs) and usually higher deposit rates. Tests practical cause-and-effect, not jargon.
B3CurrencyMultiple choice

If the rupee depreciates against the US dollar, it means:

Correct answer
A. A weaker rupee = more rupees per dollar, making imports (oil), foreign education and travel more expensive, and feeding into inflation.
B4Real returnMultiple choice

A client is pleased that her fixed deposit pays 7% while inflation is running at about 6%. Roughly speaking, is her money really growing in real terms, and by how much?

Correct answer
A. Real return ≈ return minus inflation (about 1%), and FD interest is taxable, so the post-tax real return can be near zero. This is exactly the "is an FD really safe for growing wealth?" insight she'd need to explain to clients. A tougher, applied question.
Section C

Client & Personal Finance

5 questions
C1AdvisoryMultiple choice

Risk profiling a client is mainly about:

Correct answer
B. Capacity and willingness to take risk, matched to suitable investments. Distinguishing "can afford" from "comfortable with" is a sophisticated answer.
C2Applied allocationMultiple choice

A 35-year-old client with a steady income, an emergency fund already in place, and a 15-year goal, who is comfortable with moderate ups and downs. As a starting point, which broad asset mix is most appropriate?

Correct answer
B. Long horizon + moderate risk + emergency fund in place → an equity-tilted but diversified mix. Tests applying allocation to a real profile, not just defining it. A is too timid for a 15-year goal; C and D are undiversified bets.
C3ComplianceMultiple choice

KYC ("Know Your Customer") is required mainly to:

Correct answer
B. Identity verification and anti-money-laundering. She should know this cold from client servicing — a quick one that confirms her existing ground.
C4Planning orderMultiple choice

Before putting a client's money into market-linked investments for growth, which of these should generally be in place first?

Correct answer
B. An emergency fund / liquidity buffer comes before investing for growth, so the client never has to sell investments at a bad time for a surprise expense. A core planning fundamental.
C5LiquidityMultiple choice

Which of these is generally the most liquid (easiest to convert into cash quickly)?

Correct answer
B. Cash / liquid funds convert instantly. Real estate is the least liquid. Liquidity awareness is key for emergency-fund and goal planning.
Section D

Market Awareness

4 questions
D1IndicesMultiple choice

The two main benchmark stock-market indices in India are:

Correct answer
B. Sensex (BSE) and Nifty 50 (NSE). Anyone following Indian markets should know this instantly.
D2Do you follow markets?Short answer

Roughly, around what level is the Nifty 50 trading these days? An approximate range is completely fine.

How to grade
The point is whether she follows the market at all, not pinpoint accuracy. Accept any answer in a sensible ballpark of the current level. ⚠ Interviewer: check the actual Nifty level on the day before administering, and grade against that. A confident, roughly-right number signals genuine interest; "no idea" is honest but tells you she does not track markets yet.
D3Do you follow markets?Short answer

And roughly where is the BSE Sensex trading these days? Again, an approximate range is fine.

How to grade
Same idea: it is about whether she tracks the market, not precision. The Sensex sits far above the Nifty (very roughly 3x), so a sound answer should be clearly larger than her Nifty figure. Quoting a Nifty-like number for the Sensex (or the reverse) suggests she does not really follow the indices. ⚠ Interviewer: check the actual Sensex level on the day and grade against it. Consistency between her two figures is itself a good signal.
D4CuriosityWritten

Name one financial or economic news story from the last few months that you found interesting, and in a sentence or two, say why it caught your attention.

What to look for
No fixed answer. You are testing genuine curiosity and awareness. Any real, recent, correctly-described story is a great sign. Reward someone who can explain why it matters, not just name it. A blank or a clearly made-up story is the signal to note.
Section E

On the Job

5 to diagnose
E1Behavioural risk · diagnoseAnalysis

A long-term client with a balanced portfolio and a 10-year goal wants to liquidate everything into fixed deposits during a sharp market fall. Setting the client conversation aside: what is wrong with this move analytically, and what is the sound course of action?

What to look for · the issue + the fix
Issue: selling into a fall turns a temporary, on-paper loss into a realised one; it is market timing, and missing the handful of best recovery days badly damages long-run returns; FDs may not beat inflation, so over a 10-year horizon he trades short-term volatility for near-certain erosion of purchasing power; the balanced portfolio already fits the goal. Fix: stay invested and hold the plan; if anything, rebalance to target weights (the fall may call for buying equity, not selling it); revisit the allocation only if the goal or risk profile genuinely changed, not because prices moved. Reward correct diagnosis plus a disciplined, rules-based response.
E2Risk & horizon · diagnoseAnalysis

A 58-year-old retiring in 2 years proposes putting his entire retirement corpus into a small-cap fund because it doubled last year. Analytically, what is wrong with this, and what allocation would actually be appropriate?

What to look for · the key analytical question
Issues: horizon mismatch (2 years is far too short for small-caps, which can fall 30–50% and take years to recover); sequence-of-returns risk is acute right at retirement, a big drawdown just before he starts withdrawing can permanently impair the corpus; "doubled last year" is past performance, not predictive, and small-caps are the most volatile segment; everything in one fund is extreme concentration. Appropriate: a capital-preservation tilt for the near-term needs (debt / short-duration / FDs), a modest diversified equity sleeve for the longer-dated portion, with small-caps at most a small satellite, if at all. Reward spotting horizon + sequence risk + concentration and proposing a sensible mix.
E3Cost drag · analysisAnalysis

Two similar equity funds run effectively the same strategy; one has an expense ratio of 0.5%, the other 1.5%. How much does that 1% gap actually matter over the long run, and what else would you weigh before preferring the cheaper one?

What to look for · cost vs the full picture
The 1% is charged every year on the whole corpus and compounds, so over 15–20 years it can quietly cost a large slice of the final value; cost is one of the few things known in advance and a fairly reliable predictor of relative performance, so cheaper usually wins all else equal. But not cost alone: weigh consistency and risk-adjusted returns, active vs index (a cheap index fund vs a genuinely skilled active manager), tracking error, fund size and liquidity, and fit with the goal. Strong answer grasps the compounding drag and that cheapest is not automatically best. (Exact maths not needed.)
E4Trade-offs · analysisAnalysis

For a typical retail investor, what are the real trade-offs between a diversified mutual fund and a self-managed direct-stock portfolio? Lay out the analytical case on each side.

What to look for · a balanced case
Fund: instant diversification (less single-stock / idiosyncratic risk), professional research and management, can invest small amounts via SIP, far less time and skill needed, but costs an expense ratio and you accept the manager's calls. Direct: no fund fee, full control, scope to outperform and to harvest tax losses, but needs real skill, time and temperament, and tends toward concentration and behavioural errors; most retail investors underperform. Strong answer weighs both honestly and concludes it depends on the investor's expertise, time and corpus, not a one-sided pitch.
E5Diversification · analysisAnalysis

A portfolio holds no gold because "it has done nothing for years." What role does gold actually play in a portfolio, and is a flat stretch a good reason to hold none?

What to look for · diversifier logic
Gold's job is diversification and risk reduction, not return generation: it has low-to-negative correlation with equities and tends to hold up or rise when stocks or the rupee fall, so it cushions drawdowns and smooths the ride; it hedges inflation, currency weakness and tail / uncertainty events. A flat standalone stretch is not a good reason to drop it, judging a diversifier on its own return misses the point; what matters is its effect on the whole portfolio's risk-adjusted return. Usually a small allocation. Reward understanding correlation / ballast over chasing returns.
