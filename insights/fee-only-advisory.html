Why fee-only advisory beats commission-based — a transparency primer

For high-net-worth families navigating wealth across generations and jurisdictions, the structure of advisor compensation matters more than most realise. The model your advisor operates under shapes every recommendation you'll receive — and most clients only discover this years into the relationship.

By SELEQT Editorial

The hidden architecture of commissions

Most retail and mid-tier wealth management in India operates on a commission model — the advisor earns when you buy a product. Mutual fund distributors receive trail commissions from AMCs. Insurance agents receive front-loaded payouts from insurers. Structured product manufacturers pay placement fees to anyone willing to introduce them.

None of this is illegal. Most of it is even disclosed in the fine print of documents nobody reads. But the cumulative effect is significant: when an advisor's livelihood depends on whether you buy product A versus product B, the conversation about which option is genuinely better for you becomes harder to have honestly — even when both parties intend goodwill.

The conflict isn't always dramatic. It rarely shows up as the advisor pushing an obviously bad product. More often, it surfaces as quiet biases: the higher-commission ULIP recommended over a low-cost term plan. The actively-managed fund recommended over the cheaper index alternative. The AIF placed because the manufacturer is offering a launch incentive that month.

Multiply these small biases across a twenty-year relationship and the cost to the client — measured in fees paid, returns foregone, and capital trapped in unsuitable structures — can run into several crores for a meaningful portfolio.

What "fee-only" actually means

A fee-only advisor receives payment directly from the client, and only from the client. No commissions. No trail fees. No referral kickbacks. No revenue-sharing arrangements with product manufacturers.

The fee is paid as a transparent percentage of assets under advisory, as a flat retainer, or — for episodic work like succession structuring — as a project fee. The arrangement is documented and disclosed upfront.

Critically, this means: when a fee-only advisor recommends an index fund over an active fund, a term plan over a ULIP, or a direct equity allocation over a structured product, the recommendation costs the advisor money in lost commission. Yet the advisor recommends it anyway, because the advisor's compensation is no longer linked to the product.

The distinction is sometimes muddied by terms like "fee-based" — which is not the same as fee-only. Fee-based advisors charge a fee and also accept commissions. The label sounds similar; the conflict structure is entirely different.

SEBI has formalised this distinction through the Registered Investment Adviser (RIA) framework. An RIA cannot accept commissions for the same client they advise. The wall between advice and distribution is regulatory, not merely ethical.

Why this matters more for cross-border families

For Indian residents with modest portfolios, the cost of commission-based advice is real but manageable. The dynamics change significantly for NRIs and cross-border families, for three reasons.

First, product complexity multiplies. Cross-border portfolios involve structures the average commission-based advisor isn't compensated to recommend even when they're appropriate — irrevocable trusts, holding companies in efficient jurisdictions, treaty-optimised investment vehicles, direct international custody arrangements. A fee-only advisor has no reason to steer you away from these toward higher-commission domestic products.

Second, timing dictates value. A returning NRI has a narrow RNOR window — typically two to three financial years — during which their global income receives favourable tax treatment. The right structuring within that window can preserve crores in tax efficiency. Commission-based advisors are not compensated to advise on not selling anything during this window. Fee-only advisors are.

Third, misalignment compounds across geographies. A single unsuitable product recommendation in a domestic portfolio is a localised error. The same mistake in a cross-border structure can trigger reporting obligations, tax inefficiencies, and succession complications across multiple jurisdictions. The economic cost of biased advice scales with complexity.

Five questions every prospect should ask

Before engaging any wealth advisor, fee-only or otherwise, a serious prospect should ask five questions. The answers — and the willingness to put them in writing — tell you more than any brochure ever will.

  1. How are you compensated for the advice you give me? A direct, single-source answer is what you're listening for. "Various sources" or "it depends" are signals to keep asking.
  2. If I follow your recommendation, do you receive any payment from any party other than me? This catches the fee-based muddle.
  3. Are you a SEBI-Registered Investment Adviser? RIA status is verifiable on SEBI's public registry. The registration number should be in their disclosure documents.
  4. Will you provide a written conflicts-of-interest disclosure before we sign anything? A fee-only firm will. Anything else is opaque by design.
  5. If I asked you to recommend the cheapest possible solution to a given goal, would you? A fee-only advisor's economics aren't impaired by recommending low-cost solutions. A commission-based advisor's are.

The model is the message

The advisor compensation model is not a back-office detail. It is the single most important determinant of whether the advice you receive over the next decade will be shaped by your interests or by someone else's revenue line.

For families whose wealth spans generations, jurisdictions, and asset classes, the alignment created by fee-only structure isn't a nice-to-have — it's the foundational condition for trust. Without it, every recommendation lives under the question of who else benefited.
